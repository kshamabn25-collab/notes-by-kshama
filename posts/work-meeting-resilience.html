The Project Didn’t Fail in the Meeting

A quiet story about where projects actually begin to go wrong.

The project didn’t fail in the meeting. That’s just where it became visible.

In one project, the issue was deceptively small. We missed noting down a dependency.

It wasn’t dramatic. No one ignored their work. No one consciously cut corners.

A task depended on an input from another stream. Both teams had timelines. They just weren’t aligned.

On paper, each plan made sense on its own. Together, they didn’t.

Because the dependency wasn’t explicitly captured, it never showed up as a risk. There was nothing to track. Nothing to escalate.

Weeks passed. Each team moved forward, assuming the other was aligned.

When the dependent task finally came due, the input wasn’t ready.

That’s when the questions started. Why wasn’t this flagged? Why didn’t we see this coming?

Looking back, the signs were there. They just never crossed paths in the same conversation.

The miss wasn’t in execution. It was in articulation.

A dependency not written down becomes a dependency no one owns.

And a dependency no one owns rarely becomes a risk — until it becomes a delay.

The project didn’t break because of a missed task. It broke because of a missed connection.

That experience changed how I work.

Now, I pay closer attention to what sits between plans — the handoffs, the assumptions, the quiet “we thought they had it.”

Most project issues don’t start as problems. They start as unnamed dependencies.
